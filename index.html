The Mould That Built Civilisations

WHAT IS
KOJI?

Aspergillus oryzae. A food-safe mould with a two-thousand-year history at the centre of East Asian cuisine. Without it, there is no miso, no soy sauce, no sake, no mirin, no doenjang.

Koji works by secreting a dense toolkit of enzymes — amylases, proteases, peptidases — that break down starches into sugars and proteins into free amino acids. One amino acid in particular: glutamate. The molecule of umami. The reason food tastes like more of itself.

Western chefs discovered koji's transformative power in the 2010s through Noma's fermentation lab and David Chang's Momofuku. Jeremy Umansky's 2020 book Koji Alchemy — endorsed by Sandor Ellix Katz and called "remarkable" by Noma's David Zilber — opened the door to a new generation of fermenters. Food media now calls koji "the next great fermentation wave." The condiment category hasn't caught up yet. That's where we come in.

Steam

British barley grain is steamed and cooled to exactly 35°C — the temperature at which A. oryzae thrives.

Inoculate

Koji spores (tane koji) are hand-dusted over the grain and worked in. The mould takes hold within hours.

Incubate

48 hours in a controlled cedar-tray environment at 70% humidity. The mould blooms white across the surface.

Age

Salted and sealed in ceramic crocks for six to twelve months. Patience does the rest. The result is Koji Mite.

Our Story

TWO PEOPLE.
ONE JAR.

We are two young Londoners — one with a background in food science and a deep obsession with fermentation, one with a background in design and brand-building. We met over a bowl of homemade doenjang jjigae in Peckham. We agreed immediately that British food culture was ready for something it didn't know it needed yet.

"The koji moment is like the sourdough moment, but with more depth, more history, and a flavour that makes everything else taste more like itself."

We started making koji at home — first on rice, then barley, then into salted ferments that we aged in ceramic crocks in a Dalston flat. After two years of recipe development, blind tastings with London chefs, and one very successful farmers' market test run, we believe we have a product.

We are rooted in London. We source British grain. We use traditional techniques that connect directly to Korean and Japanese fermentation culture, without pretending to be something we're not: two Londoners who fell in love with a mould.
