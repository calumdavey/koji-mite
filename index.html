The good stuff

It's a mould.
A delicious mould.

Aspergillus oryzae — koji — has been transforming humble grains into extraordinary food for over 7,000 years. Without it: no miso, no soy sauce, no sake, no doenjang. It's the quiet genius behind every deeply umami thing you've ever loved.

Koji works by releasing enzymes that break proteins down into free amino acids — specifically glutamate, the molecule responsible for that deep, moreish savoury taste that makes you close your eyes for a second. It's essentially a biological flavour amplifier. The same reason Marmite is addictive, but with a much better backstory.

How we make it — in four steps

🌾

Steam British barley

We source from UK grain growers and steam it until it's perfectly cooked — soft inside, a little firm outside. The ideal koji landing pad.

🍄

Add koji spores

Tane koji — koji spores from a Japanese supplier with 300 years of experience — are dusted over the cooled grain by hand. Then we wait.

🌡️

Incubate for 48 hours

Cedar trays. Controlled temperature. Controlled humidity. The mould grows white and fluffy across the surface. It smells like mushrooms and chestnuts. It's lovely.

⏳

Salt and age for 6–12 months

Sealed in ceramic crocks with sea salt. The enzymes do their slow, patient work. The result is Koji Mite — dark, complex, and deeply, deeply savoury.

Why it works

Marmite has been on toast since 1902.

Rose & Laura

Two people,
one obsession.

We're Rose and Laura, and we've been making koji in South East London for a couple of years now. It started as a nerdy fermentation project and somehow became a company. We blame the toast.

🔬
Rose
Microbiology grad · Data scientist

Rose grew up fascinated by the invisible world of microbes. Her microbiology degree led to a career in data science, but koji pulled her back to the bench — this time a kitchen one. She runs the fermentation side, tracking every culture with the same rigour she'd apply to a dataset.

🎨
Laura
Illustrator · Restaurateur

Laura has run restaurants, designed menus people actually want to read, and illustrated food professionally for years. She brings the hospitality instinct, the eye for what makes something beautiful, and the very strong opinions about toast toppings.

"We started with a bag of barley, a repurposed drinks fridge, and a lot of evenings doing mould-related maths. The crock in the kitchen corner is now a permanent fixture. The neighbours have questions."
