## 1. The Product

### What is Koji?

*Aspergillus oryzae* — known as koji — is a food-safe mould that has underpinned East Asian food culture for over two millennia. Used to ferment miso, soy sauce, sake, mirin, doenjang and amazake, it works by secreting enzymes (amylases, proteases, peptidases) that break down the starches and proteins in grain and legume substrates into free sugars and amino acids — particularly glutamate, the compound responsible for umami.

Koji is, chemically speaking, a biological flavour concentrator. It is also the subject of serious culinary revival: Noma's fermentation lab, David Chang's Momofuku, and Jeremy Umansky's 2020 book *Koji Alchemy* (described by critics as "the most important fermentation publication since Sandor Katz") have together triggered a wave of Western interest. Food media now describe koji as "the next great fermentation wave."

### The Koji Mite Spread

Koji Mite is a thick, dark, intensely savoury paste produced by:

1. **Koji-making:** British barley (sourced from Hodmedods or heritage grain suppliers) is steamed, inoculated with *A. oryzae* spores, and incubated at 30–35°C for 40–50 hours until fully myceliated.
2. **Salting and ageing:** The fresh koji is combined with salt and water and left to ferment for a minimum of 6–12 months, during which enzymatic activity concentrates umami compounds, develops colour, and builds complexity.
3. **Processing:** The aged ferment is blended, strained, and reduced to achieve a spread consistency, then jarred in amber glass.

**Flavour profile:** Deeply savoury, malty, with a rounded salt and subtle sweetness that distinguish it from the sharpness of Marmite. The Korean doenjang influence adds an earthiness and complexity. It is divisive — as Marmite is — but in a way that generates loyalty.

**Use cases:** Toast and butter. Ramen broth enrichment. Umami base for gravies, stews, soups. Dipping sauce component. Marinade for grilled meats and vegetables. As an ingredient in restaurant kitchens.

## 2. Market Opportunity

### The Fermentation Trend

The global fermented food and ingredients market was valued at USD 782 billion in 2024, projected to reach USD 1.38 trillion by 2034 (CAGR ~5.9%). Growth is driven by gut-health awareness, demand for natural flavour enhancers, and premium artisan food culture. In the UK, kombucha, kimchi, kefir, and sourdough have moved from health food shops to mainstream supermarkets in under a decade.

### The K-Food Surge

Korean food is the UK's fastest-growing food trend by sales volume. Key data points (2024–25):
- Korean food exports to the UK grew **122% year-on-year** in 2024
- Waitrose searches for gochujang up **572%** in 2025 vs. prior year
- Waitrose reported **71% sales growth** for gochujang
- **64.1% of British consumers** willing to buy Korean food products — the highest in Europe
- Korean restaurant listings on Just Eat up **76%** since 2024
- Major multiples (Tesco, Sainsbury's, Waitrose, M&S) now stock Korean staples

### The Gap in the Market

Despite this momentum, there is no branded koji condiment in the mainstream UK market. Existing products are:
- Japanese imports (Marukome, Hanamaruki) — specialty grocery only, no brand narrative
- Small artisan producers (The Koji Fermenteria, Kultured Deli) — markets only, limited distribution
- No product is positioned as a British-made, daily-use koji spread with mass-market ambition

This is the gap Koji Mite fills.

### Why the Marmite Comparison Works

Marmite and koji-based spreads share the same chemistry: both derive their intensity from free glutamates (yeast extract in Marmite; enzymatically-released amino acids in koji). Both are thick, dark, divisive, and deeply savoury. Both have long shelf lives. Marmite has achieved 100%+ UK household penetration on the back of exactly this positioning — embracing its polarising nature as a brand asset rather than a liability.

Koji Mite can inherit this positioning while offering something Marmite cannot: a fermentation wellness story, a Korean culinary heritage, a British provenance narrative, and a product that actively participates in the most significant food culture shift of the 2020s.

## 7. Regulatory Pathway

The principal regulatory question is whether Koji Mite constitutes a **novel food** under the UK Assimilated Regulation (EU) 2015/2283, administered by the Food Standards Agency (FSA).

**Our position:** Traditional koji products (miso, soy sauce, shio koji) have been consumed in the UK for decades via imported Japanese and Korean products, establishing precedent for the safety of *Aspergillus oryzae* fermented products. We believe a koji-fermented barley spread is analogous to existing precedented products. Umami Chef is already operating as the "first UK-approved koji producer," demonstrating a viable regulatory pathway exists.

**Immediate actions:**
1. Pre-application consultation with FSA Regulated Products team (free service, strongly recommended)
2. Registration as a food business with the relevant Local Authority (required before trading, free)
3. HACCP plan development (mandatory under Food Hygiene Regulations 2013)
4. Allergen labelling compliance (barley = gluten; soybean = soy — major UK allergens requiring declaration)
5. Nutritional information panel (required for pre-packaged retail products)

We have budgeted £3,000 for regulatory advice in Year 1.
