# Executive Summary

Koji Mite is a London-based artisan food startup producing a koji-fermented umami spread: a thick, dark, deeply savoury paste made from British barley and *Aspergillus oryzae* (koji), inspired by Korean doenjang and Japanese shio koji tradition, and made for the British kitchen.

We are founded by **Rose** (microbiology graduate, data scientist) and **Laura** (illustrator, restaurateur), both based in Peckham, SE15. We have spent two years developing the recipe; production begins in earnest in May 2026, with first sales targeted for Q4 2026.

**The opportunity in brief:**

- There is no branded, shelf-stable koji condiment in UK mainstream grocery
- Korean food is the fastest-growing cuisine in British retail (exports to UK up 122% in 2024)
- The global fermented food market is growing at 5.9% per year, driven by gut-health and natural flavour trends
- The "Marmite comparison" is chemically sound and strategically compelling: both products derive intense umami from free glutamates; both are thick, divisive, deeply savoury spreads. Marmite has proven the category. We offer something newer, more interesting, and more timely.

# 1. The Product

## What is Koji?

*Aspergillus oryzae* --- known as koji --- is a food-safe mould with an estimated 7,000-year history at the centre of East Asian food culture. Without it, there is no miso, soy sauce, sake, mirin, or doenjang. It works by secreting enzymes (amylases, proteases, peptidases) that break the starches and proteins in grain substrates into free sugars and amino acids --- principally glutamate, the compound responsible for umami.

Koji is a biological flavour concentrator. It is the reason miso tastes more complex than salt and beans alone; it is the reason soy sauce has depth that no synthetic product replicates. The enzyme leucine aminopeptidase II alone accounts for approximately 80% of the glutamic acid released from soybean proteins. Free glutamate is what koji produces in abundance. Free glutamate is what makes food taste intensely, addictively of itself.

Western professional kitchens discovered koji meaningfully in the 2010s through Noma's fermentation lab and David Chang's Momofuku empire. Jeremy Umansky's 2020 book *Koji Alchemy* --- with a foreword from Sandor Ellix Katz and praise from Noma's David Zilber --- opened koji to a new generation of Western food producers. Food media now describes koji as "the next great fermentation wave." The condiment category in the UK has not yet caught up.

## The Koji Mite Spread

Koji Mite is produced in three stages:

1. **Koji cultivation:** British barley is steamed, cooled to 35°C, inoculated with *A. oryzae* tane koji spores, and incubated for 48 hours at 30--35°C and 70% relative humidity in cedar trays until fully myceliated.
2. **Salting and ageing:** The fresh koji is combined with sea salt and sealed in ceramic crocks for 6--12 months depending on SKU. Enzymatic activity continues throughout, concentrating umami, developing colour, and building complexity.
3. **Processing and jarring:** The aged ferment is blended, strained to a consistent spread texture, and jarred in amber glass. **Shelf life: 18--24 months at ambient temperature** (enabled by salt content and low water activity).

**Flavour profile:** Deeply savoury, malty, and rounded, with a quiet sweetness that distinguishes it from Marmite's sharper yeast-extract profile. The doenjang influence adds earthiness and body. It is divisive in the way good condiments are --- in a way that builds loyalty.

**Key uses:** Toast and butter. Ramen and noodle broth. Umami base for gravies, stews, and soups. Marinade for grilled meat and vegetables. Ingredient in professional kitchens.

**Competitive moat:** The 6--12 month fermentation window means any fast-follower product is inherently 6--12 months behind. Brand development, recipe refinement, and category education (the work of getting consumers to understand and love koji) compound further lead over time. We are also applying for UK trademark registration for the **Koji Mite** brand name.

# 2. Market Opportunity

## The Fermentation Trend

The global fermented food and ingredients market was valued at USD 782 billion in 2024, projected to grow to USD 1.38 trillion by 2034 (CAGR 5.9%). Growth is driven by gut-health awareness, demand for clean-label natural flavour enhancers, and premium artisan food culture. In the UK, kombucha, kimchi, kefir, and sourdough have moved from health food specialists to mainstream multiples in under a decade.

## The K-Food Surge

Korean food is the UK's fastest-growing food trend by retail sales volume. Key data (2024--25):

- Korean food exports to the UK: **+122% year-on-year** in 2024
- Waitrose gochujang searches: **+572%** in 2025 vs. prior year
- Waitrose gochujang unit sales: **+71%** year-on-year
- **64.1%** of British consumers willing to buy Korean food products --- highest in Europe (DCMS, 2023)
- Korean restaurant listings on Just Eat: **+76%** since 2024
- Tesco, Sainsbury's, Waitrose, M&S, and Ocado now all stock Korean staples

## The Gap in the Market

Despite this momentum, there is no branded koji condiment in UK mainstream grocery. Existing products are confined to specialty channels:

- **Japanese imports** (Marukome shio koji, Hanamaruki liquid shio koji): stocked at Sous Chef and specialist Asian retailers; no brand narrative, no accessibility positioning
- **UK artisan producers** (The Koji Fermenteria, Kultured Deli, Umami Chef): farmers' market distribution only, no retail presence or mass-market ambition
- **No product** is positioned as a branded, British-made, daily-use koji spread aimed at the mainstream grocery shopper

Koji Mite fills this gap. It is the first product to combine:

- British provenance (local grain, UK production)
- Korean fermentation heritage (doenjang-inspired ageing)
- A mass-market condiment format (jarred spread, ambient stable, accessible price point)
- A coherent brand narrative (Rose and Laura, Peckham, fermentation science as story)

## Why the Marmite Comparison Works

Marmite and koji spreads share identical underlying chemistry: both derive their umami intensity from free glutamates (yeast autolysis in Marmite; enzymatic proteolysis in koji). Both produce thick, dark, shelf-stable pastes with concentrated savoury flavour. Both are polarising --- which, as Marmite's 120-year brand history demonstrates, is an asset rather than a liability.

Koji Mite can inherit Marmite's cultural positioning while offering what Marmite cannot: a live fermentation wellness story, Korean culinary heritage, British-grown grain provenance, and a product that is native to the most significant food culture moment of the 2020s.

# 7. Regulatory Pathway

Koji Mite must satisfy UK food law before trading. The regulatory landscape is manageable, with a clear pathway established by existing UK koji producers.

**Novel food status.** The principal question is whether Koji Mite constitutes a *novel food* under UK Assimilated Regulation (EU) 2015/2283, administered by the Food Standards Agency (FSA). Our position: *Aspergillus oryzae* fermented products (miso, soy sauce, shio koji) have been sold and consumed in the UK for decades via imported Japanese and Korean products, establishing precedent for the safety of the mould. Umami Chef Ltd is documented as the first UK-approved koji producer, demonstrating that a domestic regulatory pathway exists and has been navigated successfully. We will pursue a pre-application consultation with the FSA Regulated Products team (a free service available to food businesses) in May 2026 to confirm our position before trading.

**Immediate regulatory actions (timeline: May--September 2026):**

1. Register as a food business with the London Borough of Southwark (required before trading; free; minimum 28 days before first sale)
2. FSA pre-application consultation re: novel food status
3. Develop and document a HACCP food safety management plan (mandatory under Food Hygiene Regulations 2013)
4. Obtain food business public liability insurance (minimum GBP 5 million cover; ~GBP 300--500/year)
5. Confirm allergen labelling: barley = *gluten*, soybean = *soy* (both Annex II allergens under UK FIC Regulation, requiring prominent declaration on packaging)
6. Confirm nutritional information panel (required for pre-packaged retail products under UK FIC)
7. Conduct shelf-life testing (target: 18 months at ambient)

We have budgeted GBP 3,000 for regulatory advice and GBP 1,000 for trademark registration in Year 1.
